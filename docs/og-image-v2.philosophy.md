# Cartografia Tropical

A movement of quiet Brazilian measurement.

---

**Manifesto.** Cartografia Tropical is the philosophy of evaluating a personal space the way a botanical surveyor in 1950s Rio mapped a garden — with patient instruments, marginal notations, and reverence for the living thing being observed. The home office is no longer a productivity arena; it is a habitat. The work of diagnosis is the work of a careful cartographer: the gentle inscription of numbers onto paper, the marginal coral note, the long teal line of an axis drawn under good morning light.

**Form & space.** The composition behaves as if pressed against vellum. Pages breathe. The eye is given long avenues of cream before it arrives at a number. Geometry obeys a strict modernist grid, but the grid itself is invisible — only its discipline remains. Edges are sharp. Margins are wide. The placement of every glyph has been considered the way a topographer chooses where to mark a peak. This is the product of master-level execution, of countless hours of refinement: nothing is approximate, nothing is random.

**Color.** A trinity. Teal #0E3D3F is the depth of the Atlantic forest at noon, the ink of certainty, the color of measurement. Coral #F36458 is the single bird call — used once, perhaps twice, never as ornament. Cream #f7f5f0 is the parchment, the breathable air, the patient field on which observation occurs. No shadows, no gradients pretending to be light. Painstaking chromatic calibration: each value chosen so that even a thumbnail at 160 pixels still reads as itself.

**Scale & rhythm.** A monumental headline holds the left flank like an inscription cut into limestone. To its right, a small diagnostic plate — tiny ranked rows, the kind a horticulturalist might keep in a field notebook. The contrast between giant gesture and microscopic instrument is the conceptual engine of the work. Repetition lives in the diagnostic plate: six lines, six observations, six small rectangles that build meaning through accumulation. This is what the philosophy demands — a meticulously crafted equilibrium between the loud and the quiet.

**Composition & hierarchy.** The viewer's eye is led, not herded. Wordmark at the top whispers. Headline declares. Subtitle returns to a whisper. The diagnostic plate offers itself as evidence, never proof. Information sits where it must, never where it is convenient. The result is what painstaking attention buys: a single still object that holds for as long as the viewer stays, and reveals more on the second reading.

**Typographic discipline.** A single geometric grotesque does the heavy work — Bricolage Grotesque carries the headline with the authority of a Niemeyer beam. A neutral sans (Work Sans / Instrument Sans) handles the small notations. A monospaced face — DM Mono — handles only the numerical instruments, because numbers in this movement deserve to look measured, not designed. No italic. No drop shadow. No bevel. The typography is the labor of someone at the top of their field, who has decided that restraint is the highest virtue.

**The hidden reference.** The composition borrows from the language of Brazilian modernist book design (Lygia Pape's editorial work, the catalogue plates of MAM-RJ in the early 60s) crossed with the diagrammatic clarity of Otl Aicher's pictograms for Munich '72 — but warmed by the tropics. Those who recognize the influence will feel it without naming it. Those who don't will simply experience a composition that feels true. This is the mark of master craftsmanship: that the depth is felt, not demonstrated.

— *To be executed as if to be displayed in the design archive of a Brazilian modernist museum.*
